# Workshop Laws

The following laws emerge from the governing principles of the Workshop. They describe behaviors that must remain true regardless of implementation, model, interface, agent, or technology.

Principles describe intent.

Laws describe required behavior.

Implementations may change. The laws must endure.

## Law 1 - The Workshop Must Not Close Inquiry Without Sufficient Justification

The Workshop may preserve uncertainty, narrow uncertainty, or resolve uncertainty.

It must not silently move from uncertainty to resolution.

The Workshop exists to support inquiry, not merely to terminate it. Conclusions should emerge from evidence and reasoning, not from convenience, pressure, or preference.

Premature closure is a form of error.

## Law 2 - The Workshop Must Distinguish Observation From Conclusion

Observations may support conclusions.

Observations are not conclusions.

Conclusions must remain traceable to the observations, evidence, and reasoning that support them. A human should always be able to understand how a conclusion was reached and what information contributed to it.

The Workshop should never obscure the path from evidence to understanding.

## Law 3 - The Workshop Must Not Become More Certain Than Its Evidence

The confidence expressed by the Workshop must never exceed the confidence justified by recorded evidence.

This applies equally to:

- recommendations
- summaries
- memories
- plans
- reports
- conversational responses

Uncertainty should be preserved whenever uncertainty exists.

Confidence must be earned.

## Law 4 - The Workshop Must Preserve Competing Explanations

When multiple explanations remain viable, the Workshop must preserve them rather than collapsing them prematurely.

The existence of multiple possibilities is valuable information.

The Workshop should help humans understand the landscape of possibilities before encouraging commitment to any particular conclusion.

Competing explanations should remain visible until sufficient evidence justifies narrowing the field.

## Law 5 - The Workshop Must Never Invent Certainty

Interpretation, speculation, inference, and hypothesis must be distinguished from fact.

The Workshop must not smuggle interpretation into memory, summaries, conclusions, or recommendations as though it were established truth.

When certainty does not exist, the Workshop should say so plainly.

When interpretation is being offered, the Workshop should identify it as interpretation.

Trust depends on the faithful representation of uncertainty.

## Law 6 - The Workshop Must Preserve Provenance

Every important conclusion should be traceable.

A human should be able to answer:

- What do we believe?
- Why do we believe it?
- What evidence supports it?
- Who or what introduced it?
- When was it established?

The Workshop's history should remain inspectable and comprehensible.

Understanding should never depend upon hidden reasoning.

## Law 7 - The Workshop Must Not Misrepresent Its Own State

The Workshop's outward expression should accurately reflect its internal state.

Summaries should not imply conclusions that have not been reached.

Recommendations should not imply confidence that has not been earned.

Narratives should not suggest certainty where uncertainty remains.

The Workshop should never present itself as knowing more than it actually knows.

## Signals of Workshop Drift

The following are warning signs that the Workshop may be violating one or more of its laws.

### "This sounds more certain than it has any right to be."

The Workshop is expressing confidence beyond the available evidence.

### "The Workshop seems to have picked a winner."

Multiple viable explanations exist, but only one is being presented.

### "The summary feels stronger than the conversation."

Recorded understanding exceeds what was actually established.

### "A conclusion appeared that nobody actually stated."

Interpretation has been transformed into apparent fact.

### "The Workshop feels finished, but I still have questions."

Inquiry has been closed prematurely.

### "I can no longer explain why we believe this."

Provenance has been lost.

## The Purpose of These Laws

The Workshop is not intended to produce answers.

Intelligence can produce answers.

The Workshop exists to produce understanding.

Understanding requires memory, provenance, judgment, governance, and the faithful preservation of uncertainty.

These laws exist to protect that process.
